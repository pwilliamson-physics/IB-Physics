Section A: Significant Figures

How to identify the correct number of significant figures

All non-zero digits are always significant. Zeros between two non-zero digits are always significant. Leading zeros (before the first non-zero digit) are never significant — they only show the size of the number. Trailing zeros (after the last non-zero digit) are only significant if the number contains a decimal point. Writing a number in scientific notation removes this ambiguity completely, because every digit in the coefficient is significant — which is why physicists often prefer standard form.

Section B: Estimates and Orders of Magnitude

How to estimate the dimensions of an object

Start from something you already know well — your own height (about 1.7 m), a metre ruler, a bag of sugar (1 kg), or a can of drink — and compare the unknown object to it. Round your comparison to the nearest power of ten rather than aiming for a precise value: the goal of an order-of-magnitude estimate is to get within a factor of ten of the real answer, not to be exact. Useful everyday reference points include a hand span (~0.1 m), a door height (~2 m), a paperclip's mass (~1 g), and a small car's mass (~1000 kg). This estimation approach is described in your Hodder IB Physics Course Book and the IB Physics Subject Guide, where it is an assessed skill.
