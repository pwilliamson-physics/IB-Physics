Potential Difference and Kirchhoff's Laws

From gravitational to electrical potential difference

Gravitational potential (V) is the gravitational potential energy per kilogram an object has at a point: V = g h. The difference in this quantity between two points is the gravitational potential difference.

Example: lifting an elephant (6400 kg) 3 m.

Energy = mgh = 6400 × 9.8 × 3 ≈ 190 000 J (2 s.f.)

At 0 m, V = 0 J kg⁻¹

At 3 m, V = g h = 9.8 × 3 = 29.4 J kg⁻¹

Electrical potential difference is the same idea: it is the energy change of one coulomb of charge as it moves between two points with different electrical potential. A coulomb is a defined number of electrons: 1 C = 6.25 × 10¹⁸ electrons.

Kirchhoff's potential difference law

Energy is conserved, so any complete loop an electron can travel around a circuit must have a total potential difference of zero — the energy gained from the source is exactly transferred elsewhere. (Take pd added by a source as positive, and pd across components as negative.)

Kirchhoff's potential difference law: the sum of the potential differences around any closed loop is zero.

Check your understanding: in a simple series circuit, a battery supplies +12 V. There is only one bulb in the loop. What must the pd across the bulb be?

V
+12 − V = 0 (rearrange to find V).

Check your understanding: two identical bulbs are in series across a 12 V battery. By symmetry each bulb has the same pd. What is the pd across each bulb?

V
The two pds must add to 12 V, and by symmetry they're equal.
12 = V + V (both pds are equal, so rearrange to find V).

In a parallel circuit, each branch across the same two points sees the same pd — following any loop through a single bulb still gives +12 V − 12 V = 0, even though only a fraction of the charge carriers go through each bulb. That's because pd is energy per coulomb, not total energy: a third of the electrons still each change energy by the full amount.

Kirchhoff's current law

This follows from conservation of charge: charged particles cannot be created or destroyed, so the current entering any junction must equal the current leaving it.

Kirchhoff's current law: current in = current out, at every junction.

Check your understanding: at a junction, A1 = 5 A flows in. One branch carries A2 = 3 A out. Calculate the current A3 in the other outgoing branch.

A
Current in = current out: A1 = A2 + A3.
5 = 3 + A3 (rearrange to find A3).

Using Kirchhoff's two laws with Ohm's law you can analyse any circuit in this course without memorising the separate "rules" for series and parallel you can find online:

  • Current in series stays the same at every point.
  • Current in parallel splits across each branch (in inverse proportion to resistance).
  • Potential difference in series splits across components (in proportion to resistance).
  • Potential difference in parallel is the same across every branch.
