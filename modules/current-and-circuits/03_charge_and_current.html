Charge, Current and Time

Formulae you'll need: Q = I t
I = Q / t
number of charge carriers n = Q / e, where e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
Type answers as plain numbers, to 2–3 significant figures.
Every question has a hint if you get stuck, but the full working isn't shown — talk your method through with a classmate or your teacher if you're still unsure after using the hint.

Getting started

These all use Q = I t or I = Q / t directly, with times already in seconds.

1. The current in a wire is 0.35 A. Calculate the charge that passes a point in the wire in 10 s.

C
Use Q = I × t. The time is already in seconds, so you can substitute straight in.

2. A charge of 15 C passes through a wire in 5 s. Calculate the average current.

A
Rearrange Q = I t to make I the subject.

3. What is the average current in a wire when a charge of 150 C passes in 30 s?

A
I = Q / t.

4. How long does it take for a charge of 6.0 C to pass a point in a circuit when the current is 0.5 A?

s
Rearrange Q = I t to make t the subject.

Watch your units

Same formulae, but now you'll need to convert minutes to seconds, or mA/µA to A, before you substitute.

5. The current in the same wire (0.35 A) — calculate the charge passing a point in 10 minutes.

C
Convert minutes to seconds first: 1 minute = 60 s.

6. A charge of 15 C passes in 100 s instead. Calculate the average current.

A
I = Q / t — no conversion needed here, just watch your decimal point.

7. A charge of 3500 C passes a point in 9 s. Calculate the average current.

A
I = Q / t. This one gives quite a large current — that's expected.

8. The same 3500 C passes a point in 200 s instead. Calculate the average current.

A
Same formula as question 7, different time.

9. The current in a wire is 0.72 A. Calculate the charge that passes through the wire in 4 s.

C
Q = I × t.

10. Using the same current (0.72 A), calculate the charge that passes through the wire in 7 minutes.

C
7 minutes = 420 seconds.

11. In an electron beam experiment, the beam current is 1.2 mA. Calculate the charge flowing along the beam each minute.

C
1.2 mA = 1.2 × 10⁻³ A. 1 minute = 60 s.

12. The current in a small torch bulb is 0.20 A. Calculate the total charge that passes a point in the circuit in 12 minutes.

C
Convert the 12 minutes to seconds before using Q = I t.

13. In an old-fashioned television tube (a CRT), the picture is formed by a stream of electrons hitting the screen, with a current of 20 mA. How much charge hits the screen in 30 minutes?

C
20 mA = 0.020 A. 30 minutes = 1800 s.

14. How long does it take for a charge of 48 C to pass a point in a circuit if the current is 36 mA?

s
Convert 36 mA to amps before you divide.

Bigger problems

Multi-part questions, and some less familiar numbers.

15. A certain type of rechargeable battery can deliver a current of 0.2 A for 4000 s before its voltage drops and it needs recharging.

a) Calculate the total charge the battery can deliver before it needs recharging. C
b) How long could it be used for without recharging if the current through it were 0.5 A? s
c) How long could it be used for at a current of 0.1 A? s
First find the total charge in part (a). Then use that fixed value of Q in Q = I t for parts (b) and (c), rearranged to find t.

16. In a lightning flash, a typical amount of charge reaching the Earth is 10 C. If the flash lasts for 0.50 ms, what is the average current?

A
0.50 ms = 0.50 × 10⁻³ s. Expect a very large answer — lightning currents really are huge.

Counting electrons

A coulomb is a huge number of individual electrons. To count them, find the charge Q first, then use n = Q / e, where e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.

17. How many electrons pass a point in a wire in 10 minutes when the current is 1.0 µA?

electrons
Find Q = I t first (watch the µA and the minutes), then divide by e.

18. How many electrons pass a point in a wire in 10 minutes when the current is 5.0 A?

electrons
Same method as question 17, much bigger current.

19. For the 1.2 mA electron beam (question 11), how many electrons pass along the beam each minute?

electrons
You already found the charge for this one in question 11 — now divide by e.

20. How many electrons would be needed to provide the 10 C of charge in the lightning flash (question 16)?

electrons
n = Q / e, using the 10 C given in the question.

21. How many electrons passed through the wire carrying 0.72 A in 4 s (question 9)?

electrons
Use your answer to question 9 as the charge Q.

22. How many electrons passed through the wire carrying 0.72 A in 7 minutes (question 10)?

electrons
Use your answer to question 10 as the charge Q.

23. How many electrons make up a charge of 3500 C?

electrons
n = Q / e directly — you're given Q already.

24. How many electrons pass this point in the torch bulb circuit in 12 minutes (question 12)?

electrons
Use your answer to question 12 as the charge Q.

25. Calculate the number of electrons passing a point in a wire in 1 minute when the current is 1 A.

electrons
Find Q for 1 A over 60 s first.

26. Calculate the number of electrons passing a point in a wire in 1 minute when the current is 5.0 µA.

electrons
5.0 µA = 5.0 × 10⁻⁶ A. Find Q first, then n = Q / e.

27. Calculate the number of electrons passing a point in a circuit with a current of 4.0 A for 60 s.

electrons
Q = I t first, then n = Q / e.

28. Calculate the number of electrons passing a point in a circuit with a current of 12 mA for 25 s.

electrons
12 mA = 12 × 10⁻³ A.

Extension: beyond the worksheets

29. What is a semiconductor? What is a superconductor? Find a reliable source (your Hodder textbook or a trustworthy science website) and note down where your definition came from.

A semiconductor's resistivity sits between a conductor and an insulator, and changes a lot with temperature. A superconductor loses all electrical resistance below a very low "critical" temperature. Make sure you mention both ideas and give your source.
