What is resistance?

Resistance is a measure of how difficult it is to make a current pass through a component — it resists the flow of charge and so reduces the current for a given pd.

This matters for how we design measuring instruments too: a connecting wire should have almost zero resistance (so it doesn't waste energy or limit the current anywhere else in the circuit); an ammeter sits in series, so it also needs almost zero resistance, or it would change the very current it's trying to measure; a voltmeter sits in parallel, so it needs a very high resistance, so that almost no current takes the "shortcut" through the meter instead of through the component being measured.

Ohm investigated the relationship between potential difference and current, and found that for a conductor at constant temperature, current is proportional to pd: V ∝ I, or V = IR. The constant of proportionality, R, is the resistance — a property of that specific component's physical characteristics, under those particular conditions (constant temperature).

How are resistance and temperature linked? — the Drude model

Drag the slider to add thermal energy to the right-hand wire and compare it with the reference wire on the left — watch how a hotter lattice scatters the tracked electron (white-ringed dot) more often. (credit Dr Jones, https://www.drjonesphysics.com/electricity-apps1)

In a metal, the positive ions are fixed in the lattice — the electric field pushes on them too, but they can't accelerate off anywhere, so no net work goes into their kinetic energy. Electrons, by contrast, are free to move: the field does work on them and they gain kinetic energy. That's why it's the electrons that carry the current.

As temperature rises, the ions vibrate more. They get in the way of the drifting electrons more often, slowing them (resisting their motion) — this is why a metal's resistance increases with temperature.

Paul Drude (1900) adapted the kinetic theory of gases to the free electrons in a metal, treating the ions as fixed in the lattice. He suggested the electrons' random thermal motion was around 100,000 m s⁻¹ — far faster than the drift velocity caused by the field, which is typically only around 0.0001 m s⁻¹. This drift velocity is a tiny average bias on top of the random motion, and it's what we call the current. Because the ion spacing limits how far an electron can travel before colliding with one, the electrons can't continuously accelerate — instead they repeatedly scatter, with an average time between collisions called the relaxation time, Δt.

Putting the force equation together with E = V/L and the drift-velocity relationship leads back to Ohm's law, V = IR — where R depends on Δt. Δt is set by the temperature (via the random thermal speed), so changing the temperature changes Δt, which changes the resistivity and therefore the resistance. At constant temperature, Δt (and so resistivity and resistance) stays constant.

A more modern (quantum) picture describes electrons in a perfectly periodic lattice as Bloch electrons, which can move without any resistance at all. Real resistance comes from electrons scattering off disturbances in the lattice: vibrations (phonons), impurities, and defects. Drude's "collisions" were really an early classical stand-in for these interactions. As temperature rises, the lattice vibrates more strongly, there are more scattering events, Δt falls, and resistivity rises — electrical resistance is the macroscopic result of moving electrons transferring energy to the vibrating crystal lattice.

If electrons in a metal move randomly at around 100,000 m s⁻¹, why doesn't the current keep changing direction?

The random motion really is random and averages to zero net displacement on its own. But the electric field superimposes a tiny drift velocity (~0.0001 m s⁻¹) in one direction on top of that random motion. Averaged over huge numbers of electrons, that small bias is the current — and it's steady because the field is steady, even though each individual electron is still mostly moving randomly.

What affects resistance?

The resistance of a component or wire depends on four factors — three geometric/material properties, plus temperature (which we covered above):

1. Resistivity, ρ (measured in Ω m): the material's intrinsic opposition to the flow of charge. Copper is a better conductor than steel, so copper has a lower resistivity than steel.

2. Length, L (measured in m): in a longer wire, charge carriers have to collide with more ions, so a higher voltage is needed to push the same current through — long wires have high resistance.

3. Cross-sectional area, A (measured in m²): in a thick wire, less voltage is needed to push the charge carriers through — thick wires have low resistance.

4. Temperature: as covered above, a hotter lattice vibrates more and scatters electrons more often, increasing resistance (for a metal).

Combining the geometric factors: R = ρL / A.
