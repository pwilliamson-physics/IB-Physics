EMF and Internal Resistance

Part A — Notes: emf and internal resistance

emf and potential difference

In a circuit there are components that put energy into the circuit, and components that take energy out of it.

A device putting energy into a circuit is providing an electromotive force (emf). A device taking energy out of the circuit has a potential difference (p.d.) across it. Both emf and p.d. are measured in volts, V.

  • Emf, ε — the total energy transferred (theoretical maximum) per coulomb of charge that passes through the source.
  • Potential difference — the energy transferred out of the circuit per coulomb of charge.

Terminal p.d. and "lost volts"

When a cell is supplying no current, the potential difference across the cell (its terminal p.d.) is equal to its emf. Once the cell starts supplying current, some of the emf is "lost" — dissipated through heating the cell.

As the current increases further (I = 0.4 A), the terminal p.d. drops again (V = 1.3 V, so p.d. lost = 0.2 V). The larger the current, the more potential difference is "lost" inside the cell.

Internal resistance

An electrical cell is made from metals and chemicals which themselves have some resistance. This is called the internal resistance of the cell, r.

This resistance is small, so it is given the symbol "little r."

Inside the cell, energy is put into the circuit by the emf (ε), but some of this energy is taken back out by the internal resistance before it ever reaches the rest of the circuit. So the p.d. available to the rest of the circuit is:

available p.d. = emf − p.d. lost internally

Using Ohm's law: emf = p.d. across R + p.d. across r, and since the same current I flows through both:

ε = IR + Ir      ε = I(R + r)

Finding emf and r from a graph

Rearranging ε = I(R + r) in terms of the terminal p.d., V (where V = IR):

ε = V + Ir      V = −Ir + ε

This has the same form as a straight-line graph, y = mx + c, if we plot V (y-axis) against I (x-axis):

  • gradient, m = −r (so the internal resistance is the magnitude of the gradient)
  • y-intercept, c = ε (the emf is the value of V when the graph is extrapolated back to I = 0)

Worked examples

Example 1. What is the terminal p.d. for a cell of emf 2 V and internal resistance 1 Ω when it is connected to a 9 Ω resistor?

ε = IR + Ir = I(R + r)

2 = I(9 + 1)

I = 2/10 = 0.2 A

V = IR = 0.2 × 9 = 1.8 V

So this 2 V cell actually supplies only 1.8 V to the external circuit.

Example 2. Find the internal resistance of the cell if the emf is 2 V, the current is 0.2 A and R = 9 Ω.

ε = I(R + r)

2 = 0.2(9 + r)

r = 1 Ω

Example 3. The p.d. across the terminals of a cell is 3.0 V when it is not connected to a circuit (no current flowing). When connected to a circuit with a current of 0.37 A flowing, the terminal p.d. falls to 2.8 V. What is the internal resistance of the cell?

ε − V = Ir

3.0 − 2.8 = Ir

r = 0.2 / 0.37 = 0.54 Ω

Your turn

1. A current of 0.5 A flows when a cell of emf 6 V is connected to an 11 Ω resistor. What is the internal resistance of the cell?
r = Ω
ε = I(R + r)

6 = 0.5(11 + r)

12 = 11 + r

r = … Ω
2. A 12 V cell with internal resistance 1 Ω is connected to a 23 Ω resistor. What is the p.d. across the 23 Ω resistor?
V = V
I = ε/(R + r) = 12/(23 + 1) = 0.5 A. V = IR = 0.5 × 23 = … V
3. A cell of emf 12 V and internal resistance r = 1.5 Ω produces a current of 3.0 A. What is the potential difference across the cell terminals?
V = V
V = ε − Ir = 12 − (3.0 × 1.5) = 12 − 4.5 = … V
4. When two 4.0 Ω resistors are connected in parallel with a cell, the current leaving the cell is 3.0 A. When the same two resistors are connected in series with the cell, the total current is 1.4 A. Calculate the internal resistance of the cell.
r = Ω
Parallel: R = (4×4)/(4+4) = 2 Ω, so ε = 3.0(2 + r) = 6 + 3r.

Series: R = 4+4 = 8 Ω, so ε = 1.4(8 + r) = 11.2 + 1.4r.

Setting equal: 6 + 3r = 11.2 + 1.4r

1.6r = 5.2

r = … Ω.
4 (continued). Using your value of r, calculate the emf of the cell.
ε = V
ε = 6 + 3r = 6 + 3(3.25) = … V (check: ε = 11.2 + 1.4r = 11.2 + 1.4(3.25) gives the same value)

A cell of emf 12 V and internal resistance r = 2 Ω is connected to two resistors, X = 6 Ω and Y = 6 Ω, in parallel.

ε = 12 V, r = 2 Ω
X = Y = 6 Ω
Current through cell = A
Current through each branch = A
X ∥ Y = (6×6)/(6+6) = 3 Ω

ε = I(R + r)

12 = I(3 + 2)

I = … A (current through the cell)

Since X = Y, the current splits equally between the two branches: … A each.

Self-assessment questions

1. A typical hand-held torch runs off two 1.5 V cells (in series), yet has a lamp rated 2.5 V, 0.5 A. What is the internal resistance of each cell (assume they are identical)?
r (each cell) = Ω
Total emf = 2 × 1.5 = 3 V

ε = V + Ir

3 = 2.5 + (0.5 × rtotal)

rtotal = 0.5/0.5 = 1 Ω (for both cells), so r per cell = 1/2 = … Ω.

(The 0.5 V "lost" is shared between the two cells.)
2. A typical car battery has an emf of 12 V and must provide a current of 80 A to the starter motor. If the internal resistance is 0.05 Ω, find the potential difference across this internal resistance when the starter motor is running.
Vr = V
Vr = Ir = 80 × 0.05 = … V ("lost volts"). The car battery needs a very low internal resistance so it can supply this high current without losing too much terminal p.d. Starting the car with the headlights on dims them, because the extra 4 V lost internally leaves only 8 V for the lights instead of the full 12 V.
3. Some school laboratories have EHT (Extra High Tension) power packs giving up to 3000 V. For safety, they are provided with a 50 MΩ resistor in series with the supply. What is the maximum current that could be drawn from the supply? Give your answer in μA.
Imax = μA
Maximum current happens when the external resistance is 0 (short circuit): Imax = ε/r = 3000/(50×10⁶) = 6 × 10⁻⁵ A = … μA. A torch bulb of 10 Ω connected across this supply would only receive about I×R ≈ 6 × 10⁻⁴ V — the 50 MΩ resistor dominates completely, which is exactly why it's there for safety.
4. A student experimenting with a solar cell connects a 1000 Ω voltmeter across it and reads a p.d. of 1.0 V. Using an (almost) infinite-resistance digital voltmeter instead, the reading is larger: 1.2 V. What is the internal resistance of the solar cell?
r = Ω
The low-resistance voltmeter allows a small current to flow, so it reads less than the true emf; the near-infinite-resistance voltmeter draws (almost) no current, so it reads the true emf ≈ 1.2 V.

With the 1000 Ω meter: I = V/R = 1.0/1000 = 0.001 A.

Lost p.d. = 1.2 − 1.0 = 0.2 V = Ir

r = 0.2/0.001 = … Ω.
5. A 9.0 V battery has an internal resistance of 12.0 Ω. (a) What is the potential difference across its terminals when it is supplying a current of 50.0 mA?
V = V
V = ε − Ir = 9 − (50×10⁻³ × 12) = 9 − 0.6 = … V
5 (continued). (b) What is the maximum current this battery could supply?
Imax = A
Maximum current occurs when R = 0: Imax = ε/r = 9/12 = … A. (c) A sketch of terminal p.d. V against supplied current I would be a straight line sloping downward from V = ε (at I = 0) with gradient −r — so the internal resistance can be found from the magnitude of the gradient.
6. When a cell is connected directly across a high-resistance voltmeter, the reading is 1.50 V. When the cell is shorted through a low-resistance ammeter, the current is 2.5 A. What is the internal resistance of the cell?
r = Ω
emf = 1.5 V (high-resistance voltmeter draws almost no current, so reads the true emf).

Imax = 2.5 A occurs when R = 0 (short circuit), so Imax = ε/r

r = ε/Imax = 1.5/2.5 = … Ω.

In the circuit below, a variable resistor is connected to a battery of emf ε.

The graph shows how the reading of the voltmeter (in V) varies with current (in A) as the variable resistance changes. Use the graph (extrapolating the dashed line where needed) to determine:

a the internal resistance of the battery.
r = Ω
b the emf of the battery.
ε = V
c the maximum current that can be established in this circuit.
Imax = A
Reading two points from the line, approximately (I = 0.5 A, V = 5.5 V) and (I = 3.0 A, V = 1.5 V):
a gradient = (1.5 − 5.5)/(3.0 − 0.5) = −4.0/2.5 = −1.6, so r = |gradient| = … Ω.
b extrapolating back to I = 0 (the y-intercept): ε = V + Ir = 5.5 + (1.6 × 0.5) = … V.
