Section 1 – Current in Series and Parallel Circuits

What is current?

'The flow of electrons' is a standard definition, but it is vague and can lead to confusion. At IB level we say:

Current is the amount of charge passing a point per second.

The unit of current is the Ampere (A) and the unit of charge is the Coulomb (C):

1 A = 1 C s−1

Each electron carries a charge of 1.6 × 10−19 C, so 1 A of current means:

6.25 × 1018 electrons per second

So current is the flow of electrons, but it is a precise number of electrons per second for every ampere.

Worked Example & Practice – Number of Electrons

Example: How many electrons are there in 20 Coulombs?

Number of electrons = total charge ÷ charge of one electron
= 20 ÷ (1.6 × 10−19)
= 1.25 × 1020 electrons

The Equation: I = ΔQ / Δt

I = ΔQ / Δt

where I = current (A), ΔQ = charge transferred (C), Δt = time interval (s).

💡 Tip: Remember to convert minutes and hours into seconds before using I = ΔQ / Δt. 10 min = 600 s; 10 hours = 36 000 s.

Why Does Current Travel So Fast?

Individual electrons actually move very slowly through a wire (a few millimetres per second). However, when electrons move they push their neighbours, like balls in a tube. The effect travels at close to the speed of light, even though the electrons themselves drift slowly.

This is why a light turns on the moment you flick the switch.
